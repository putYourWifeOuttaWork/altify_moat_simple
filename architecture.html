The architecture layer

Under the coach, under the loop, there is a machine.

Enterprise selling runs as three layers. Sellers execute on top. Humans and AI collaborate in the middle. And underneath sits the architecture layer: everything but humans. This page is about the bottom of the cake, because everything above it stands on what happens down here.

“Structure is never inferred. Values are inferred once, then ratified. Answers are retrieved, not guessed.”

the quarantine law: inference is allowed only at the edges, never in the middle.

Pillar one · Ontology

Every reference carries its why.

A cross-reference without meaning still needs a guess: the system knows A relates to B, but not why. In our structure the why is stored inside the reference. The maps give the rubrics their subjects. The rubrics give the maps their meaning. Each explains the other, and the structure adapts by rule: which methodology, which questions, which process, chosen by the deal's own attributes, not by inference.

Pillar two · Traversal

One ID. Everything walkable.

Everything about a deal hangs off one opportunity ID in typed relationships. AI walks a branch and the answer is already there. And because the schema is closed, it can do the one thing retrieval cannot: enumerate what is missing.1

Pillar three · Algorithmic

The structure is dynamic. The computation is absolute.

Scores, roll-ups, and which-value-wins are computed by code, the way they have been here for a decade. Not because algorithms are old, but because where a right answer exists, guessing at it is malpractice.2

Where there's a right answer, we compute it.

The output · Determinism

Determinism is not a pillar. It is the product.

Ontology gives meaning. Traversal gives reach. Algorithm gives computation. Together they print one guarantee at the boundary, and everything above stands on it: ask twice, get the same answer twice, and get told what's missing. In one measurement, a frontier model asked the same question a thousand times at its most careful setting gave eighty different answers.3 This layer gives one.

The fourth dimension

4D is not a fourth pillar. It is the same three, through time.

Traversal through time is walking a timeline. Ontology through time is deltas that mean something: role changed, sentiment shifted, flag raised. Computation through time is a rule deciding which value wins.2 One record per person, per deal, deepening daily instead of multiplying.4

The cost

Paid once. Amortized forever.

Designing a closed schema is tedious, and it happens at design time, once, then evolves on a cadence. That cost is real, and it is the price of admission that keeps this architecture where it belongs: deals with many stakeholders and long cycles, where guessing is the thing you cannot afford. Below that line, lighter tools are the right answer, and we say so.

Necessary above the enterprise line. Unnecessary below it. That is the point.

THE STACK, TECHNICALLY

Closed, expert-defined schema. The ontology is the customer's own methodology encoded as objects, fields, and typed relationships: relationship maps, insight maps, process steps, assessment rubrics, all children of one opportunity ID with deterministic foreign keys. Nothing about the structure is model-generated.

Data as metadata. Which methodology applies, which questions it asks, and which process governs are selected by rule from the deal's own attributes: solution, segment, motion. The structure is dynamic without being inferred.

The write edge. LLM extraction runs once per new input, at ingestion. Every extracted value carries a citation to its source and a confidence level. Low confidence routes to a human for ratification; high confidence writes with full audit. Inference exists here, quarantined, and caught.

The read edge. Answers are assembled by traversal and computation. Model inference at read time is limited to sense-making over already-true values: a fraction of the volume, and never the source of facts.

Time. Changes write as deltas onto one persistent record per entity: append-only journal beside upserted current state, with deterministic survivorship deciding which value wins, and compaction rolling old deltas into period summaries.

WHY EACH CHOICE, THE EVIDENCE

Why a closed schema: models cannot reliably detect what is absent from open text: frontier models reach only about 70% F1 finding deliberate omissions even at small context sizes, because attention cannot attend to gaps.1 A closed schema turns absence into a null-field lookup. Every red flag is exactly that.

Why computation over judgment: replacing model judgment with a deterministic rule improved accuracy by 10.8 points, widening to 21 at long context, while a leading temporal knowledge graph scored 7% on the same conflict-resolution task.2 Our survivorship and scoring are those rules.

Why not generation-time answers: identical temperature-zero sampling produced 80 distinct outputs in 1,000 runs.3 Long context degrades 30 to 50% before advertised limits, worst on cross-referencing.5 Retrieving pre-computed state uses about 90% fewer tokens than re-reading history.4

Why ratified citations: roughly half of citations generated at answer time are invalid on audit.6 Ours are written at ingestion and human-ratified where uncertain, following the proven pattern of routing the uncertain few percent to people.7

The shared weakness, said plainly: our write edge depends on the same extraction reliability that limits automatic knowledge graphs, with published error rates from a few percent to roughly a quarter in open graphs.8 Confidence routing and ratification contain it. Nothing eliminates it. The highest-value number we can ever publish is our own measured extraction precision and ratification catch rate.
