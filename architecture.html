The architecture layer

Under the coach, under the loop, there is a machine.

Enterprise selling runs as three layers. Sellers execute on top. Humans and AI collaborate in the middle. And underneath sits the architecture layer: everything but humans. This page is the bottom of the cake, a structural replica of the deal's interior, and everything above it stands on what happens down here.

“Structure is never inferred. Values are inferred once, then ratified. Answers are retrieved, not guessed.”

the quarantine law: inference is allowed only at the edges, never in the middle.
Models read. Algorithms compute. Humans ratify.
The requirements

The job makes five demands. Guessing meets none of them.

Enterprise selling is not like other AI work. A support bot can be ninety percent right across a million tickets, because the errors average out. A quarter of enterprise selling has a dozen deals that matter, so the errors concentrate. That difference writes the job's requirements, and the requirements write the architecture.

The design · One deal, three planes

The standard. The situation. The history.

Around one opportunity ID sit three planes. The rubric holds what must be true. The maps hold what is true now. The strata hold what has been true. Four typed arrows connect them, and only one of them can change anything.

A deal review, drawn as a machine.

Pillar one · Ontology

A knowledge graph, written by the methodology.

This structure is a graph: typed entities, typed relationships, one deal at the root. The difference is who writes it. Elsewhere, a model extracts a graph from conversations, so the structure records what was said. Here, the methodology declares the structure, so it records what must be true. That is why absence is computable: a red flag is a condition that must be true and isn't, and only a declared structure can say so. The maps give the rubrics their subjects. The rubrics give the maps their meaning. Which methodology, which questions, which process: chosen by the deal's own attributes, not by inference.

The ontology is calibrated per customer at design time, from their own closed-lost history: their rulebook, learned from their deals, owned in their tenant. Nothing learned ever leaves.

Their structure records what was said. Ours declares what must be true.

Pillar two · Traversal

One ID. Everything walkable.

Everything about a deal hangs off one opportunity ID in typed relationships. AI walks a branch and the answer is already there. Because the schema is closed, it can do the one thing retrieval cannot: enumerate what is missing.1 And because every entity already owns a key, incoming signals resolve by narrowing, not by matching.

Retrieval finds what was written. Traversal knows what's missing.

Pillar three · Algorithmic

The structure is dynamic. The computation is absolute.

Scores, roll-ups, and which-value-wins are computed by code, the way they have been here for a decade: where a right answer exists, guessing at it is malpractice.2 The same is true of warnings. There are three species of flag, and severity is computed, never declared.

The output · Determinism

Determinism is not a pillar. It is the product.

Ontology gives meaning. Traversal gives reach. Algorithm gives computation. Together they print one guarantee at the boundary, and everything above stands on it: ask twice, get the same answer twice, and get told what's missing. In one measurement, a frontier model asked the same question a thousand times at its most careful setting gave eighty different answers.3 This layer gives one.

The fourth dimension

Time is authored, not conjured.

No platform manufactures a timeline for you. Snapshots capture state; to know what changed and why, something must diff the frames and guess at the meaning, at read time, every time. Here the meaning is written once, at the moment it matters: when a change is ratified, the loop writes a delta. Role changed. Sentiment shifted. Flag raised. Each delta carries its source, its citation, and its ratifier, so citing history is citing a node, not a frame. The record keeps two grains: one card of current truth per entity, and an append-only journal of deltas beneath it. Old deltas compact into period summaries on a cadence.

Where it runs

An activation, not a build.

The memory layer runs on the platform's own native data engine, inside the customer's tenant. Ratified records stream in through the platform's free connector. Consolidation is a key join, because every record already shares an ID: no probabilistic matching, no unification engine, and none of its cost. The nightly pattern math runs as scheduled batch computation priced in fractions of a cent per million rows, inside an allowance most enterprise customers already hold. The expensive machinery this platform offers, profile unification and streaming, is machinery this design never turns on.

The customer's tenant. The customer's keys. The customer's meter, barely moving.

Learning · Two cadences

The rulebook amends. The playbook advises.

Two learning loops run over the ratified record, in a required order. The analysis loop mines what actually killed deals and, when a pattern clears a threshold, proposes an amendment to the rulebook: a new condition that must be true. Humans ratify it; a new version ships. The optimization loop watches what the best sellers do inside the rules and coaches everyone else, continuously, as advice. Methodology first, optimization second, because you cannot compute "better" against an undefined standard. And both loops are only trustworthy because they read ratified truth: pattern mining over guessed data compounds the guessing.

Both loops learn only from ratified truth. That is why they compound instead of drift.

The collaboration layer's one motion

One brief. Every dawn.

The layer above this machine has one daily motion: inspect and reconcile. Because flags and deltas are records, the morning brief is not a new system, it is a scheduled read: every unratified yellow, every new red, every contention on this seller's deals, ranked, narrated, and sent where that team already works. Thirty pings train the mute button. One ranked brief trains a ritual. Ratification always returns through the gate, and every brief is logged: what was sent, when, through which door.

Always on, not always available.

The steel man

The strongest alternative, drawn honestly.

The best systems in this field share one design. They capture every interaction, extract meaning with models, probabilistically match it to accounts, people, and deals, keep a persistent memory in the vendor's cloud, re-read and re-score it daily, and write updates back to the record. Every serious system now answers anywhere; access is table stakes, and the contest is what the answers stand on. This design is genuinely superior for open questions: discovering themes across unknown documents, cold-starting on unstructured text, exploring a corpus nobody modeled.9 10 Given its address, outside the system of record, it is close to the best possible design. The address is the flaw. Score it against the job's five requirements, and the failures are structural, not incidental.

For open questions over unknown corpora, grow a graph. For governed answers over a known domain, own the ontology. Enterprise sales is the second kind of job.

The cost

Paid once. Amortized forever.

Designing a declared schema is tedious, and calibrating it per customer takes design-time work with their own history. That cost is real, it happens once, and it evolves by amendment on a cadence. It is the price of admission that keeps this architecture where it belongs: deals with many stakeholders and long cycles, where guessing is the thing you cannot afford. Below that line, lighter tools are the right answer, and we say so.

Necessary above the enterprise line. Unnecessary below it. That is the point.

The numbers we owe

A protocol and a date, not adjectives.

Two numbers matter more than any claim on this page: measured extraction precision at the write edge, and the ratification catch rate, how often humans correct what the model routed to them. The measurement protocol is defined, it runs first on our own pipeline, and the first results print before September 15.

You cannot fake green. Starting with ours.

THE STACK, TECHNICALLY

Closed, expert-defined schema. The ontology is the customer's own methodology encoded as objects, fields, and typed relationships: relationship maps, insight maps, process steps, assessment rubrics, all children of one opportunity ID with deterministic foreign keys. Nothing about the structure is model-generated. The structure is a graph in the strict sense: typed nodes, typed edges, one root. It is declared, versioned, and admin-extensible; instances are dynamic, the schema changes only by amendment.

Data as metadata. Which methodology applies, which questions it asks, and which process governs are selected by rule from the deal's own attributes: solution, segment, motion. Fifty divisions can run fifty calibrated rulebooks in one tenant. The structure is dynamic without being inferred.

The write edge. Extraction runs once per new signal, at ingestion, behind a resolution funnel: deterministic keys narrow first (the seller's address bounds the deal set, calendar attendees key most voices), closed-set selection assigns the rest (the model picks from the deal's mapped people, a list, not a world), and the truly ambiguous residue routes to a human with a citation. People mentioned but unknown are never probabilistically merged: the system proposes a new stakeholder, a human ratifies, and the identity question is answered once, forever. Every extracted value carries a citation and a confidence level. Low confidence routes to ratification; high confidence writes with full audit. Inference exists here, quarantined, and caught.

The read edge. Answers are assembled by traversal and computation. Model inference at read time is limited to sense-making over already-true values: narrating why a computed flag exists, assembling the morning brief. A fraction of the volume, and never the source of facts.

Time, in two grains. Each entity keeps one profile card of current truth, upserted, and one append-only journal of ratified deltas beneath it, each delta carrying source, citation, ratifier, and timestamp. Deterministic survivorship decides which value wins. Compaction rolls old deltas into period summaries on a cadence, so the record deepens without hoarding. Consolidation across an entity's many per-deal cards is a key join plus a materialized entity view: the platform's profile-unification engine is never enabled, because deterministic foreign keys make it unnecessary, and its cost, orders of magnitude above batch computation, is never incurred.

The memory layer, concretely. Ratified records stream to the platform's native data engine inside the customer's tenant over the free CRM connector. Pattern analysis runs as scheduled batch calculated insights, priced per million rows scanned11; threshold conditions fire native triggered flows that create recommendation records, post to chat, or feed an agent. Weekly and monthly cadences keep consumption inside the free allowance most enterprise editions already include12. Streaming computation and profile unification are excluded from the design on purpose. Generative narration, where used, runs through the platform's governed AI gateway; the batch layer is tenant-local by architecture and never leaves.

The delivery edge. The brief subsystem is one-way publication of rendered content, never record sync: a preference registry (asked at design time: where does this team work), a per-timezone assembler that runs after the nightly write completes, channel adapters (email and chat natively, other suites via governed webhook), and a ledger logging every brief sent: content, time, door. Agents pull the same brief over the open assistant protocol instead of being pushed. Ratification from any door returns through the governed loop.

Packaging. Platform rules require the memory-layer metadata to ship as its own package beside the existing application package, distributed as a data kit; the pattern is the same one the platform vendor uses for its own industry kits. The core loop requires no custom code beyond the assembler, the adapters, and the write edge.

WHY EACH CHOICE, THE EVIDENCE

Why a closed schema: models cannot reliably detect what is absent from open text: frontier models reach only about 70% F1 finding deliberate omissions even at small context sizes, because attention cannot attend to gaps.1 A closed schema turns absence into a null-field lookup. Every red flag is exactly that. And in this vertical, absence detection is the job: qualification is the discipline of finding what's missing.

Why a normative schema: a structure extracted from conversations can only describe what was said. A structure declared by methodology prescribes what must be true, which is the only way a machine can compute a red flag, and the only way it can compute a contention: the present disagreeing with the ratified past. Descriptive stores cannot raise either, because they contain no obligations and no ratified history to contend with.

Why computation over judgment: replacing model judgment with a deterministic rule improved accuracy by 10.8 points, widening to 21 at long context, while a leading temporal knowledge graph scored 7% on the same conflict-resolution task.2 Our survivorship, scoring, and escalation are those rules. Severity is computed from role, transition, and dwell time, never declared by a model.

Why not generation-time answers: identical temperature-zero sampling produced 80 distinct outputs in 1,000 runs.3 Long context degrades 30 to 50% before advertised limits, worst on cross-referencing.5 Retrieving pre-computed state uses about 90% fewer tokens than re-reading history.4

Why ratified citations: roughly half of citations generated at answer time are invalid on audit.6 Ours are written at ingestion and human-ratified where uncertain, following the proven pattern of routing the uncertain few percent to people.7

Why closed-set resolution: open-set identification, matching a name against the world, is where probabilistic systems spend fortunes and still err; the field's patent portfolios are monuments to it. Closed-set selection, picking one of nine mapped people, is where extraction models are strong and hallucination has nowhere to land. The schema converts the hard problem into the easy one before any model runs.

Why authored time: snapshots preserve state and discard meaning; recovering "what changed and why" from frames requires re-inference on every read, forever. Writing the delta once, at ratification, with its citation and its ratifier, makes history cheap to carry and free to cite. Fixed lookback windows in interaction-corpus systems are the tell of the opposite cost model: when every question re-reads the past, the past must be capped.

Why per-tenant learning: the rulebook loop calibrates each customer's ontology from their own closed-lost history, at design time, and amendments ship as versions after human ratification. Nothing is trained across customers, and nothing learned leaves the tenant. Cross-tenant learning builds the vendor's asset; per-tenant learning builds the customer's.

The honest graph comparison: emergent graph retrieval is real progress and wins real jobs: multi-hop discovery and thematic summarization over unknown corpora are its documented strengths.9 The same benchmark literature shows it underperforming plain retrieval on simple factual and time-sensitive questions at multiplied latency,10 and the field's own consensus now pairs graphs with deterministic computation in front of structured data. That hybrid conclusion is this page's design, stated from the other direction.

The shared weakness, said plainly: our write edge depends on the same extraction reliability that limits automatic knowledge graphs, with published error rates from a few percent to roughly a quarter in open graphs.8 Confidence routing, closed-set narrowing, and ratification contain it. Nothing eliminates it. The highest-value numbers we can publish are our own measured extraction precision and ratification catch rate, and the protocol for both is defined with a date.
