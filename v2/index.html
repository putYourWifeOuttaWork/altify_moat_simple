WHY DEAD DEALS FUND THE WHOLE THING · the evidence

The largest study of sales conversations ever run, 2.5 million recorded calls, found that 40 to 60 percent of qualified deals end in no decision, and that these deals consume seller time all the way through the process.7 Late-stage is worse: over 20 percent of forecasted deals, the ones that already survived qualification, die in no decision. Deals left open past twice the average sales cycle close at a 3 percent rate. Failing faster is not a defeat; it frees capacity for deals that can win.

Qualification rigor is the highest-leverage behavior in selling: full use of a MEDDPICC-style rubric lifted win rates 311 percent across 3.2 million opportunities, high performers were 437 percent more likely to complete their qualification criteria, and having just two criteria complete by the end of stage two made deals 71 percent more likely to close.8 Yet only 15 percent of opportunities get fully qualified by hand. That gap is precisely what daily AI red flags close.

The learning loop pays too: 63 percent of companies with win/loss programs report win-rate gains, rising to 84 percent once the program is two years old (vendor survey), and a 2014 Gartner note estimated up to 50 percent win-rate improvement from comprehensive win/loss analysis (dated estimate, labeled as such).9

Why the grace period: clinical alert-fatigue research shows acceptance of a reminder drops roughly 30 percent for each additional alert in the same encounter, and about 10 percent for every 5 percent of alerts that are repeats.10 Coaching that fires constantly stops being heard. Ours is paced by design.

The math that ties it together: sales velocity equals qualified opportunities, times win rate, times deal size, divided by cycle length. Killing dead deals early moves two of the four levers at once: the honest win rate rises and the effective cycle shortens, while the recovered hours refill the top of the formula.
